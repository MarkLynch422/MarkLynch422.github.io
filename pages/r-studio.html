Project 1 — Global Cellular Subscription Analysis

Analyzed World Bank and ITU mobile subscription data covering 192 countries from 2000–2022 to identify income, regional, and time-based predictors of global cellular technology adoption. This was a team project (4 members) completed in ISyE 312 — Data Management & Analysis for Industrial Engineering.

Research Questions

  • Does national income level predict mobile subscription rates, and how has that relationship changed from 2000 to 2022?
  • Does geographic region have a significant independent effect on subscription rates?
  • Are lower-income countries growing their subscription rates faster than higher-income ones?
  • Is there statistical evidence of beta-convergence — low-subscription countries catching up to high-subscription ones?

Methods & Results

All analysis was conducted in R using base statistical functions and ggplot2 for visualization. The dataset required significant cleaning due to historical reporting gaps, political border changes, and uneven global infrastructure rollout.

  • Simple Linear Regression (Income vs. Subscriptions): Run separately for 2000 and 2022. In 2000: slope = 13.67, R² = 0.43, p ≈ 0. In 2022: slope = 20.34, R² = 0.31, p ≈ 0. Model assumptions validated via QQ plots and Cook's distance — identified and addressed influential outlier countries.
  • Single-Factor ANOVA (Regional Effects): Applied to 2022 data. Result: p = 0.00419, confirming region has a statistically significant effect on subscription rates independent of income level.
  • Growth Rate Regression: Regressed year-over-year subscription growth against income. Slope = −6.264, p = 0.002 — significant direction confirmed, but R² = 0.0023, meaning income explains almost none of the variance. Infrastructure and urbanization are the real drivers.
  • Beta-Convergence Analysis: Tested whether low-subscription countries are catching up. Slope = −123.22, p = 0.155 (not significant), R² = 0.0105. Convergence trend exists but is not income-driven.

Key Finding

A clear income–subscription relationship exists, but geographic region and infrastructure "leapfrogging" — countries skipping landline buildout and moving directly to mobile — better explain why lower-income regions are closing the gap faster than income alone would predict.

Tools Used

  • Base R — regression (lm), ANOVA (aov), residual diagnostics
  • ggplot2 — scatter plots, QQ plots, regression overlays, Cook's distance plots
  • Manual data cleaning — handling reporting gaps, political boundary changes, outliers

Project 2 — Student Performance Analysis

Conducted hypothesis tests, confidence intervals, and linear regression on a public dataset of 1,000 student exam scores to evaluate the impact of test preparation and parental education on performance. ISyE 210 — Intro to Industrial Statistics, team project (5 members).

Methods & Results

  • Descriptive Statistics: Computed mean, variance, std dev, range, and proportions across all 9 data components. Selected math scores, parental education level, and test prep completion for deeper analysis.
  • Hypothesis Testing (5 tests): One-sample z-tests on mean math scores vs. national SAT benchmark, parental education level, and reading scores vs. benchmark. One-sample z-test on test prep completion rate vs. national benchmark. Two-sample z-test comparing math scores between test-prep and non-test-prep groups.
  • Confidence Interval: 95% CI for mean math score: [65.15, 67.03]. Confidence level selected based on explicit precision vs. interval width tradeoff analysis.
  • Simple Linear Regression (Manual Derivation): Regressed math score on test prep completion. Slope = 5.62, intercept = 64.08, R² = 0.0316. SSE, SST, and variance of random error computed by hand. Concluded weak fit; multiple regression with additional predictors would perform better.

Ethical Considerations

The analysis explicitly addressed Type I/Type II error implications and the ethics of using demographic predictors (parental education, lunch status) to forecast individual student performance — a dimension often omitted in introductory work.
